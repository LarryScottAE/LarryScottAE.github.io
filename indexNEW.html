Wikipedia MIDI Definition
MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a technical standard that describes a communications protocol, digital interface, and electrical connectors that connect a wide variety of electronic musical instruments, computers, and related audio devices for playing, editing, and recording music. A single MIDI cable can carry up to sixteen channels of MIDI data, each of which can be routed to a separate device. Each interaction with a key, button, knob or slider is converted into a MIDI event, which specifies musical instructions, such as a note's pitch, timing and loudness. One common MIDI application is to play a MIDI keyboard or other controller and use it to trigger a digital sound module (which contains synthesized musical sounds) to generate sounds, which the audience hears produced by a keyboard amplifier. MIDI data can be transferred via MIDI or USB cable, or recorded to a sequencer or digital audio workstation to be edited or played back.

Scott Stompbox Prototype
This stompbox gives a musician MIDI foot control of audio effects while performing. This stompbox is particulaly well suited for use with audio software such as Reaper, ProTools, CakeWalk,and Cubase. With this stompbox, you can also control a Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) equipment such as a keyboards and/or synthesizers. For example, with the press of a switch, you can set the patch and effects on any DAW. Lastly, this stompbox can be used to control lighting functions using Midi Show Control. Press a footswitch to change any lighting scene setting.    To assign switch Midi functions, download (registered users only) the Windows based stompbox control program, connect the stompbox to your PC using a USB cable, launch the program, and assign 'Program Change', 'Continuous Control' and/or 'System Exclusive' commands to the individual switches. The stompbox provides multiple configuration 'maps' allowing the user to toggle between multiple groups of switch functions in real time.

A typical configuration is to use the stompbox to control Reaper playback of rhythm and bass tracks when developing a lead or jamming. To minimize disruption of the creative process, jumping between verse markers, chorus markers, and bridge markers needs to be seamless. A particularly useful configuration is set the bottom row of 6 switches to jump between chorus, verse, and bridge markers in an audio project, with the next row of 6 switches used to enable / disable tracks during playback. For example, you may want to work with different rhythm tracks while working out a lead. Just press the stompbox switch to toggle between different rhythm tracks. Reaper has a particularly nice feature called 'smooth seeking' that advances to the next marker/section on completion of the current marker/section. The top row of 4 switches is used to control start/stop/record, tempo increase/decrease, loop on/off, and toggle between midi maps. Since all of the switches are independently configurable, you can setup the stompbox to best suit your individual needs.

I am confident that this stompbox's performance competes well with any commercially available stompbox. One sad fact I learned building this stompbox prototype is; the raw materials (alone) that are necessary to build this stompbox are more expensive than a fully assembled, commercial unit from many stompbox providers. This fact alone will likely prevent me from mass producing this stompbox, but it does provide useful information for other DIY guitar accessory

Scott Stompbox Features:
  • Sixteen independently configurable foot switches per map.
  • Toggle between four distinct configuration maps in real time.
  • Send MIDI program change, continuous control, and system exclusive commands.
  • Midi transmit and receive function via standard midi cables or USB cable. (midi yoke).
  • Windows USB based program used to define configuration maps and switch functions.
  • Compatible with all major digital analog workstations (DAWs).
  • Switches can be configured as latch (toggle on/off) or pulse function.
  • Configuration map and switch function shown in display upon switch press.
  • Atmel 8-bit AVR microprocessor (ATmega168) dedicated to monitoring footswitch press.
  • Atmel ARM Cortex-M3 CPU (SAM3X8E) dedicated to supervisory functions.
  • AdaFruit Micro SD (Micro SD) memory card holds up to 16 gig of config.
  • A large 4 line,20 character,Hantronix blue backlit display.
  • Free Custom Reaper 'Action List' to demonstrate the Reaper capabilities
  • Rugged 16 gauge sheet metal enclosure can take a lot of abuse!
  • Powered by standard 12 volt / 2 amp wall adapter

Scott Stompbox Connections:
  • USB connection to Windows PC for realtime programming & configuration.
  • Power connector providing 12 volt /2 amps of power.
  • MIDI input plug to receive remote MIDI instructions (such as map changes).
  • Midi output plug to send MIDI commands to your DAW.
  • USB connector can also be used as a Midi input/output using 'MIDI Yoke'.

Be aware that MIDI cables, although not as fast as a MIDI USB connection, are designed to be run over long lengths. A midi cable can easily run 100+ feet without any signal degredation issues. By contrast, Midi over a usb cable using Midi Yoke is limited to the USB cables. USB cables typically experience signal degredation when the USB cable exceeds six feet. A third alternative (where I had had good experience) is using a MIDI USB cable

Scott Stompbox Brains
An Arduino Due microprocessor is the primary controller for the Scott Stompbox (blue board in picture). It's primary job is to control stompbox input and output.

The Scott interface baord (yellow board in picture) contains a voltage requlator, midi interface circuitry, 16 gig Micro SD card, 5 volt to 3 volt level shifters for midi interfaces and display output), Atmel 8-bit AVR microprocessor (ATmega168) dedicated to monitoring footswitch press. 16 channel CD4067 multiplexer, to monitor switch press, and two 74HC595 8 output shift registers (to drive the LED's above each switch).

Arduino Due features include:
  • Atmel SAM3X8E ARM 32-bit CPU
  • CPU clock rate of 84Mhz.
  • 96 KBytes of static RAM.
  • 512 KBytes of flash memory (code memory).
  • 54 digital input/output pins.
  • 4 UARTs (hardware serial ports).
  • USB-serial port on the SAM3X chip.
  • A DMA controller to relieve CPU of memory intensive tasks.
  • ** 98% of today's cell phones contain ARM processors.

Scott Stompbox CASE:
The Scott Stombox case was fabricated in my garage using a Baliegh 36 inch sheet metal brake, a Miller Spectrum 375 plasma cutter, an Ingersol Rand 50 gallon / 120 CFM air compressor, and a Sear's 20,000 rpm air gun sander.

The case in made of 16 guage sheet metal and is approximately 24 inches wide by 10 inches height. Front depth is 1 inch and rear depth is 2 inches. The weight of the stompbox is roughly 8 lbs. Even the most aggressive stomping will not dent this case !

Primer base coat is DupliColor 'self etching primer'. The final coats are Krylon black expoxy Refrigerator paint. (I experimented with Duplicolor black enamel, but, given my weak paint skills, I got a smoother and tougher finish with Krylon expoxy paint).
